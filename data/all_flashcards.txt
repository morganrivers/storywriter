Explicit vs. implicit diffusion[sound:flashcard_front67.mp3]	1‑D heat eqn \( \partial_t T = \kappa\,\partial_{xx}T \).<br><b>FTCS</b> (explicit): \( T_i^{n+1}=T_i^{n}+r(T_{i+1}^{n}-2T_i^{n}+T_{i-1}^{n}) \) with \( r=\kappa\Delta t/\Delta x^{2} \); stable only if \( r\le\tfrac12 \).<br><b>Backward‑Euler</b> (implicit): same stencil at \(n+1\); unconditionally stable ⇒ larger \( \Delta t \) but requires solving a tridiagonal system.[sound:flashcard_back67.mp3]
Crank–Nicolson scheme[sound:flashcard_front68.mp3]	Semi‑implicit, second‑order: \( T_i^{n+1}=T_i^{n}+r\frac{(T_{i+1}^{n}-2T_i^{n}+T_{i-1}^{n})+(T_{i+1}^{n+1}-2T_i^{n+1}+T_{i-1}^{n+1})}{2} \). **A‑stable** (\(|G|\le1\)), but for large \(r\) it can leave undamped 2‑Δx “checkerboard” oscillations.[sound:flashcard_back68.mp3]
Von Neumann (Fourier) stability test[sound:flashcard_front69.mp3]	Assume trial solution \( \phi_i^{\,n}=G^{n}e^{ik i\Delta x} \). Insert into discretised PDE to get amplification factor \(G(k\Delta x)\). Scheme is stable if \(\lvert G\rvert\le1\) for all wave numbers in \([0,\pi]\). Provides quick linear stability criterion without solving PDE analytically.[sound:flashcard_back69.mp3]
Centred‑in‑space advection instability[sound:flashcard_front70.mp3]	FTCS for \( \partial_t \phi + c\,\partial_x\phi = 0 \): \( \phi_i^{n+1}=\phi_i^{n}-\frac{\mathrm{Co}}{2}(\phi_{i+1}^{n}-\phi_{i-1}^{n}) \) with Courant \( \mathrm{Co}=c\Delta t/\Delta x \). Von Neumann gives \( G=1-i\,\mathrm{Co}\sin k\Delta x \) ⇒ \(\lvert G\rvert^{2}=1+\mathrm{Co}^{2}\sin^{2}k\Delta x>1\) unless Co=0; thus <b>unconditionally unstable</b>.[sound:flashcard_back70.mp3]
Semi‑implicit stability idea[sound:flashcard_front71.mp3]	Split fast (e.g. gravity‑wave) terms implicitly and slow terms explicitly. For shallow‑water: treat \(g\partial_x\eta\) implicitly so amplification \(G\) satisfies \( |G|\le1 \) for any \( \Delta t \), allowing large timesteps while keeping cost moderate; proof follows from solving 2×2 linear system and showing spectral radius ≤ 1.[sound:flashcard_back71.mp3]
Taylor column[sound:flashcard_front72.mp3]	In rapidly rotating (small Ro) viscous flow past a vertical obstacle, the vorticity equation gives \( \partial_z(\nabla^{2}\psi) \approx 0 \) ⇒ horizontal streamfunction independent of \(z\). Fluid columns “stick” to the obstacle, deflecting flow vertically and forcing depth‑coherent motion (Taylor–Proudman theorem).[sound:flashcard_back72.mp3]
Shallow‑water equations[sound:flashcard_front73.mp3]	Height‑averaged system for layer depth \(h=\eta+H\) and velocity \(u=(u,v)\):<br>\( \partial_t h + \nabla\!\cdot(hu) = 0 \)<br>\( \partial_tu + (u\!\cdot\!\nabla)u + f\hat{k}\timesu = -g\nabla\eta \). Captures gravity waves, geostrophic balance, storm surges; foundation of barotropic ocean & atmospheric models.[sound:flashcard_back73.mp3]
Potential‑vorticity (PV) conservation[sound:flashcard_front74.mp3]	For shallow water: \( q = \dfrac{\zeta + f}{h} \) with relative vorticity \( \zeta = \hat{k}\!\cdot\!\nabla\timesu \). Material derivative \( \dfrac{Dq}{Dt}=0 \) (absent forcing/dissipation). PV mixing underlies Rossby‑wave dynamics and western‑boundary currents.[sound:flashcard_back74.mp3]
Rossby wave (mid‑lat β‑plane)[sound:flashcard_front75.mp3]	For **barotropic shallow‑water** on a β‑plane \( f=f_0+\beta y \) the dispersion relation is \( \displaystyle \omega = -\frac{\beta k}{k^{2}+l^{2}+1/R_{d}^{2}} \) with deformation radius \( R_{d}=\sqrt{gH}/|f_0| \). If \( H\!\to\!\infty \) (rigid‑lid) the \(1/R_{d}^{2}\) term vanishes, giving the classic dry‑Rossby formula \( \omega=-\beta k/(k^{2}+l^{2}) \).[sound:flashcard_back75.mp3]
Rossby wave prerequisites[sound:flashcard_front76.mp3]	Need meridional \(f(y)\) gradient (β≠0); vanish on constant‑\(f\) (\(f\)-plane). Restoring mechanism is potential‑vorticity conservation; pressure gradient provides inertia‑gravity restoring at short scales.[sound:flashcard_back76.mp3]
Kelvin wave (coastline)[sound:flashcard_front77.mp3]	Linear shallow‑water with a rigid coast at \(x=0\). Solution \( \eta=\eta_{0}\,e^{-|f|x/c}\,e^{i(k y-\omega t)} \) where \( c=\sqrt{gH} \). Wave is **non‑dispersive** \( \omega = ck \); amplitude decays offshore on the Rossby radius \( R_{d}=c/|f| \) and propagates with the coast on its left (NH).[sound:flashcard_back77.mp3]
Equatorial Kelvin wave[sound:flashcard_front78.mp3]	At equator (\(f\approx\beta y\)) solves equatorial β‑plane; meridional structure \(e^{-y^{2}/2R_{e}^{2}}\). Still nondispersive \( \omega=c k \), symmetric about equator, trapped within Rossby radius \(R_{e}=\sqrt{c/\beta}\).[sound:flashcard_back78.mp3]
Poincaré (inertial‑gravity) waves[sound:flashcard_front79.mp3]	Shallow‑water, constant \(f\); dispersion \( \omega^{2} = f^{2}+gH(k^{2}+l^{2}) \). **Dispersive**; approaches inertial frequency \(f\) at long wavelength and gravity‑wave limit \( \omega\approx c k \) at short scales.[sound:flashcard_back79.mp3]
Hermite polynomials in equatorial waves[sound:flashcard_front80.mp3]	Separable solutions of the linear β‑plane yield meridional structures \( \Psi_n(y) \propto H_{n}\!\bigl(y/R_{e}\bigr)\,e^{-y^{2}/2R_{e}^{2}} \) where \(H_n\) are Hermite polynomials. Provide modal families: n=0 (Kelvin), n=1,2,… (symmetric/antisymmetric Rossby–gravity modes).[sound:flashcard_back80.mp3]
Surface Ekman layer[sound:flashcard_front81.mp3]	Balance \( fu = (1/\rho)\partial_z \boldsymbol{\tau} \). Solution \( u+iv = \frac{\tau_{s}}{\rho \sqrt{2\nu f}}\,e^{(1+i)z/\delta_E} \) with thickness \( \delta_E = \sqrt{2\nu/|f|} \). Velocity turns 45° to right (NH) and decays exponentially with depth – the **Ekman spiral**.[sound:flashcard_back81.mp3]
Bottom Ekman layer[sound:flashcard_front82.mp3]	Near solid bed, viscous stress opposes geostrophic flow; spiral rotates opposite sense, causing **Ekman transport toward low pressure** and inducing upward/downward motion at top of layer.[sound:flashcard_back82.mp3]
Ekman transport[sound:flashcard_front83.mp3]	Depth‑integrated surface layer transport \( M_E = \frac{\hat{k}\times\boldsymbol{\tau}_s}{\rho f} \); 90° to the right (NH) of wind stress. Magnitude \( |M_E| = \tau_s/(\rho |f|) \).[sound:flashcard_back83.mp3]
Ekman pumping/Suction[sound:flashcard_front84.mp3]	Vertical velocity at Ekman depth: \( w_E = \frac{1}{\rho f}\,\hat{k}\!\cdot\!\nabla\times\boldsymbol{\tau}_s \). Positive curl (cyclonic wind) ⇒ upwelling; negative curl ⇒ downwelling. Drives Sverdrup circulation and nutrient supply.[sound:flashcard_back84.mp3]
Iceberg drift rule[sound:flashcard_front85.mp3]	Icebergs, embedded in surface Ekman layer, move at ~2% of wind speed and ~20°–30° to the right (NH). Vector sum of wind drag, ocean drag, Coriolis, and pressure gradient explains deviation from both wind and surface current directions.[sound:flashcard_back85.mp3]
Kelvin–Helmholtz energy argument[sound:flashcard_front86.mp3]	For two layers of depth\(h_{1,2}\) and velocities \(U_{1,2}\), perturbing the interface converts mean kinetic energy to wave potential energy. If shear is large enough, wave gains exceed gravitational restoration ⇒ instability.[sound:flashcard_back86.mp3]
Base state & Taylor‑Goldstein eqn[sound:flashcard_front87.mp3]	Start with Boussinesq \( N^{2}(z) \) stratification and shear \( U(z) \). Linearize, assume \( \propto e^{ik(x-ct)} \), eliminate pressure ⇒ **Taylor–Goldstein** ODE: \( (U-c)(\phi''-k^{2}\phi) - U''\phi + \frac{N^{2}}{U-c}\phi = 0 \). Imaginary \(c\) ⇒ exponential growth.[sound:flashcard_back87.mp3]
Richardson number criterion[sound:flashcard_front88.mp3]	Gradient Richardson number \( \mathrm{Ri}=\dfrac{N^{2}}{(\partial U/\partial z)^{2}} \). Continuous shear is unstable only if Ri < ¼ somewhere (Miles–Howard). For a two‑layer interface with reduced gravity \( g' = g(\rho_{2}-\rho_{1})/\rho_{0} \), instability requires \( \Delta U > \sqrt{g'(h_{1}+h_{2})/(h_{1}h_{2})} \).[sound:flashcard_back88.mp3]
